KARATE

The Way of the Empty Hand

What is Karate?

Karate (Japanese: "empty hand") is a traditional Japanese martial art that originally developed in the Ryukyu Kingdom (present-day Okinawa). It is predominantly a striking art utilizing punches, kicks, knee strikes, elbow strikes, and open-hand techniques such as knife-hands and spear-hands.

Like many traditional martial arts, Karate places a significant emphasis on discipline, respect, and character development. Practitioners (karateka) learn through the repetition of basic techniques (kihon), structured forms or patterns of movement (kata), and controlled sparring (kumite).

The Founder of Modern Karate

While Karate was developing over centuries in Okinawa, the art was systemized and brought to mainland Japan by a man who is widely recognized as the "Father of Modern Karate":

Gichin Funakoshi

Gichin Funakoshi (1868–1957) was an Okinawan schoolteacher who played the pivotal role of introducing Karate to mainland Japan during the early 20th century. Funakoshi studied under two prominent Okinawan masters, Anko Itosu and Anko Asato.

Funakoshi formalized his style, which came to be known as Shotokan. The name "Shoto" was Funakoshi's pen name (meaning "pine waves"), and "kan" means hall or house. Therefore, Shotokan essentially means "the house of Shoto." He firmly believed that Karate should be accessible to everyone and focused heavily on the spiritual and educational aspects of the art, not just its martial applications.

Important Note on Karate Philosophy

Gichin Funakoshi famously stated, "Karate ni sente nashi," which translates to "There is no first strike in Karate." This highlights the fundamental philosophy of Karate: it is defensively oriented and never to be used for aggressive, unprovoked attacks.

The ultimate aim of Karate lies not in victory or defeat, but in the perfection of the character of its participants.
